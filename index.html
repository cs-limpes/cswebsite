About Me

I began my journey building computers, setting up networks, and writing desktop applications.

Then I fell in love with the internet - teaching myself HTML, CSS, and JavaScript.

When I moved from desktop application development to web development, it was typical and normal for one person to handle everything on a website - from coding to design to security to marketing. I've always been a generalist and I've always loved learning new things, so I was in my element.

Over the years, these areas became specializations and they are a lot to keep up on - and naturally, I've had to let some things go. But I've held on to engineering, usability/user experience, and marketing.

Since I worked with so many small businesses and independent artists, I found myself doing a lot of business consulting as well - helping them create pricing structures and packages, hiring their teams, teaching them about payroll practices, etc. And slowly began to offer my own consultation services - and naturally, the businesses I worked with became larger, too, and I grew with them.

I primarily provide technology solutions and marketing consultations - software development, architecture, and IT strategy, as well as content strategy, assessments, SEO, growth strategy, and go-to-market strategies.

I truly believe that technology is a tool that can make lives better, easier, and more productive - taking humanity to places we only ever dreamed of - and I'm extremely excited about and proud of my contributions to that development.

Three things I stand behind:

I'm passionate about user experience and believe strongly in listening to the people using your product - external and internal. They know best how a product is being used rather than how it was intended to be used and it's essential to listen to their feedback and requests.

A truly intuitive user interface has yet to be developed - we have all been taught to use websites and applications and because they all use similar interfaces, they "feel" intuitive - but they aren't.

Accessibility is essential and the curb cut effect is real. If you ignore disabled and otherwise impaired users, your product will suffer and you will be left behind - this user group needs to be top of your list instead of an after thought.
